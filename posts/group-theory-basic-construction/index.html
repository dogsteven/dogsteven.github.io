Groups ­¤öŚ

Definition 1. A group is a set $G$ together with a binary operator $\star$ on $G$ $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \star: G\times G & \to G \\ (a, b) & \mapsto ab \end{alignat*} $$ satisfying the following axioms

  1. $\star$ is associative, i.e., for every $a, b, c\in G$ we have $(ab)c = a(bc)$.
  2. There is an element $e\in G$, called an identity, such that for every $a\in G$ we have $ae = ea = a$.
  3. For each element $a\in G$, there is an element $a^{-1}\in G$, called an inverse of $a$, such that $aa^{-1} = a^{-1}a = e$.

If $\star$ is commutative, i.e., for every $a, b\in G$ we have $ab = ba$, the group $G$ is said to be abelian or commutative.

In most cases, we call the operator $\star$ the multiplication and call $G$ a multiplicative group. If $G$ is abelian, we usually write $a + b$ instead of $ab$ and call it the addition and call $G$ a additive group.

The condition 2 ensures that a group is non-empty. It’s easy to observe that the identity of a group is unique, and the inverse of an element of a group is uniquely determined by this element.

In a multiplicative group, the indenity is denoted by $1$ and the inverse of an element $a$ is denoted by $a^{-1}$. In an additive group, the identity is denoted by $0$ and the inverse of an element $a$ is denoted by $-a$.

We write $a^n$ instead of $\underbrace{aa…a}_{n\text{ times}}$, write $na$ instead of $\underbrace{a + a + … + a}_{n \text{ times}}$, and define $a^0\overset{\text{def}}{=} 1$.

We also write $a^{-n}$ and $(-n)a$ instead of $(a^n)^{-1}$ and $-(na)$, respectively. It’s easy to see that $$a^{n + m} = a^na^m \text{ and } (n + m)a = na + ma$$ for every $n, m\in \mathbb{Z}$. Also $$\left(a^{m}\right)^{n} = a^{nm}\text{ and } n(ma) = (nm)a$$ for every $n, m\in \mathbb{Z}$. We call $a^n$ a power of $a$.

Definition 2. For a group $G$ and $a\in G$, the order of $a$, denoted $|a|$, is defined to be the cardinality of $\left\{a^n : n\in \mathbb{Z}\right\}$. If $|a|$ is finite number $n$, we say that $a$ is of order $n$. Otherwise, we say that $a$ is of infinite order.

The order of $G$, denoted $|G|$, is defiend to be the cardinality of $G$.

One observes that if $G$ is finite, every element of $G$ is of finite order.

Definition 3. A subgroup of a group $G$ is a non-empty subset of $G$ that closed under the multiplication and inverse. Formally, a non-empty subset $H$ of a group $G$ is called a subgroup of $G$ if the following properties hold

  1. For every $a, b\in H$ we have $ab\in H$.
  2. For every $a\in H$ we have $a^{-1}\in H$.

There are two trivial subgroups of a group $G$: the entire $G$ and the identity subrgroup $\{1\}$, denoted by $1$ ($0$ in additive groups).

We write either $H\leqslant G$ or $G\geqslant H$ to express the fact that $H$ is a subgroup of $G$. One can make an observation that a subgroup of a group is also a group under the same operator. If $H$ is a subgroup of $G$ and $H\ne G$, we say that $H$ is a proper subgroup of $G$ and write either $H < G$ or $G > H$.

Proposition 1. For a group $G$, the relation $\leqslant$ is a partial ordering on the set of all subgroups of $G$. The union of a chain of proper subgroups is also a proper subgroup. Therefore, every group has a maximal subgroup.

The itersection of a chain of non-identity subgroups need not to be a non-identity subgroup of $G$: Consider the group $\mathbb{Z}$ under addition and the chain $\left\{2^n\mathbb{Z}\right\}_{n\in \mathbb{N}}$ where $$a\mathbb{Z} = \left\{ak : k\in \mathbb{Z}\right\}$$ Then we have $$\bigcap\limits_{n = 0}^{\infty} 2^n\mathbb{Z} = \left\{k\in \mathbb{Z} : (\forall n\in \mathbb{N})(\exists a\in \mathbb{Z})(k = 2^n\cdot a)\right\} = 0$$

Proposition 2. The intersection of a collection of subgroups of a group $G$ is also a subgroup of $G$.

Definition 4. Let $A$ be a subset of a group $G$. The subgroup generated by $A$ in $G$, denoted $\left<A\right>$, is defined to be the smallest subgroup of $G$ containing $A$. Formally, $$\left<A\right> \overset{\text{def}}{=} \bigcap\limits_{\substack{A \subseteq H \\ H\leqslant G}} H$$

Proposition 3. Let $G$ be a group, let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$ and let $A$ be a subset of $H$. Then the subgroup generated by $A$ in $H$ is as the same as the subgroup generated by $A$ in $G$.

Theorem 1. Let $A$ be a subset of a group $G$. Then $$\left<A\right> = \left\{a_1^{\varepsilon_1}a_2^{\varepsilon_2}…a_n^{\varepsilon_n} : n\in \mathbb{Z}^{+}, a_i\in A, \varepsilon_i\in \{1, -1\}\right\}$$

Definition 5. For a group $G$ and $a, g\in G$, the conjugate of $a$ by $g$ is defined to be $gag^{-1}$. For a subset $A$ of $G$, define $$gAg^{-1} := \{gag^{-1} : a\in A\}$$ called the conjugate of $A$ in $G$.

Definition 6. For a group $G$ and a non-empty subset $A$ of $G$, define the centralizer of $A$ in $G$ by $$C_G(A) \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{g\in G : gag^{-1} = a \text{ for every }a\in A\right\}$$ and the normalizer of $A$ in $G$ by $$N_G(A) \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{g\in G : gAg^{-1} = A\right\}$$

For an element $g\in G$, we say that $g$ centralizes (resp, normalizes) $A$ if $g\in C_G(A)$ (resp, $g\in N_G(A)$). For a non-empty subset $B$ of $G$, we say that $B$ centralizes (resp, normalizes) $A$ if $B\subseteq C_G(A)$ (resp, $B\subseteq N_G(A)$).

Proposition 4. For a group $G$ and a non-empty subset $A$ of $G$, the centralizer and normalizer of $A$ are subgroups of $G$. Furthermore, $C_G(A)\leqslant N_G(A)\leqslant G$.

Definition 7. A subgroup $H$ of $G$ is said to be normal in $G$ (or is a normal subgroup of $G$) if $gHg^{-1} = H$ for every element $g\in G$. In this case, we write $H\trianglelefteq G$ or $G\trianglerighteq H$.

In another words, a subgroup $H$ of $G$ is normal in $G$ if and only if $N_G(H) = G$.

Proposition 5. A subgroup $H$ of $G$ is normal in $G$ if and only if for every $h\in H$ and $g\in G$, $$ghg^{-1}\in H$$ In another words, $H$ is normal in $G$ if and only if $gHg^{-1}\subseteq H$ for every $g\in G$.

Proposition 6. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. Then the normalizer of $H$ in $G$ is the largest subgroup of $G$ in which $H$ is normal.

Definition 8. For $A$ and $B$ subsets of a group $G$, define $$AB \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{ab : a\in A, b\in B\right\}$$

Proposition 7. Let $H, K$ be finite subgroups of a group $G$, then $$\left|HK\right| = \dfrac{|H|\cdot |K|}{|H\cap K|}$$

Proof. Given $k_1, k_2\in K$, suppose that $k_1H = k_2H$, then $k_1^{-1}k_2\in H$ so $k_1^{-1}k_2\in H\cap K$. Therefore, the set $$A = \{kH : k\in K\}$$ contains $\dfrac{|K|}{|H\cap K|}$ cosets of $H$. In another hand, $$HK = \bigcup\limits_{k\in H} kH = \bigsqcup\limits_{kH\in A} kH$$ so $$|HK| = |H|\cdot|A| = \dfrac{|H|\cdot |K|}{|H\cap K|}$$

Proposition 8. Let $H, K$ be subgroups of a group $G$. Then $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$ if and only if $HK = KH$.

Proof. Suppose that $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$. Given $h\in H, k\in K$, we have $$kh = \left(h^{-1}k^{-1}\right)^{-1}\in HK$$ so $KH \subseteq HK$. On the other hand, $$(hk)^{-1} = k^{-1}h^{-1}\in KH$$ so $HK = (HK)^{-1} \subseteq KH$. Therefore, $HK = KH$.

Conversely, suppose that $HK = KH$. Given $h_1,h_2\in H, k_1, k_2\in K$, we have $$(h_1k_1)^{-1}h_2k_2 = k_1^{-1}h_1^{-1}h_2k_2 = h_3k_3k_2 \in HK$$ where $h_3\in H, k_3\in K$ such that $k_1^{-1}(h_1^{-1}h_2) = h_3k_3$. Hence $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$.

Proposition 9. Let $H, K$ be subgroups of a group $G$ such that $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$. Then $$HK = \left<H, K\right>$$

Proof. We have $H, K$ are subsets of $HK$, so $\left<H, K\right> \leqslant HK$. Obvously, $HK$ is a subset of $\left<H, K\right>$, so $HK = \left<H, K\right>$.

Corollary 1. Let $H, K$ be subgroups of a group $G$. If $K$ normalizes $H$, then $HK$ is a subgroup $G$ and $H$ is normal in $HK$.

Proof. Since $K$ normalizes $H$, for every $k\in K$ and $h\in H$, we have $$hk = k\left(k^{-1}hg\right) \in KH\text{ and }kh = \left(khk^{-1}\right)k\in HK$$ so $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$. Since $K$ normalizes $H$, we have $$HK = \left<H, K\right> \leqslant N_G(H)$$ so $H$ is normal in $HK$.

Definition 9. For $G$ a group, $H$ a subgroup of $G$ and $a$ an element of $G$, define $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} aH & \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{ah : h\in H\right\} \\ Ha & \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{ha : h\in H\right\} \end{alignat*} $$

Definition 10. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. Define a relation $\sim_H$ on $G$ as follows $$a\sim_H b\iff a^{-1}b\in H$$ This relation is an equivalence relation, whose equivalence classes are called left cosets of $H$ in $G$. The set of all left cosets of $H$ in $G$ is denoted by $G / H$.

Similarly, define a relation $\sim^{H}$ on $g$ by $$a\sim^{H} b\iff ab^{-1} \in H$$ This relation is an equivalence relation, whose equivalence class are called right cosets of $H$ in $G$. The set of all right cosets of $H$ in $G$ is (terribly) denoted by $H \setminus G$.

Proposition 10. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. If $N$ is a left coset of $H$ in $G$, then for every $a\in N$ we have $N = aH$. As a consequence, $$G / H = \left\{aH : a\in G\right\}$$ Similarly, if $N$ is a right coset of $H$ in $G$, then for every $a\in N$ we have $N = Ha$. Also $$H \setminus G = \left\{Ha : a\in G\right\}$$

Corollary 2. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. Then $$a\sim_H b \iff (\exists h\in H)a = bh \iff aH = bH$$

Corollary 3. If $G$ is a finite group and $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$ and $|G| / |H|$ is the number of left cosets (or right cosets) of $H$ in $G$.

Definition 11. The index of a subgroup $H$ of a group $G$, denoted $[G : H]$, is defined to be the cardinality of $G / H$.

Proposition 11. Suppose that $G$ is a group and $H, K$ is subgroups of $G$ such that $H \leqslant K$. Then $$[G : K]\cdot [K : H] = [G : H]$$ In particular, $|G| = [G : H]\cdot |H|$.

Proof. Let $G / K = \{g_{\alpha}K\}_{\alpha}$ and $K / H = \{k_{\beta}H\}_{\beta}$, we have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} G = \bigsqcup\limits_{\alpha} g_{\alpha}K & = \bigsqcup\limits_{\alpha}\left(g_{\alpha}\bigsqcup\limits_{\beta} k_{\beta}H\right) \\ & = \bigsqcup\limits_{\alpha, \beta} (g_{\alpha}k_{\beta})H \end{alignat*} $$ Thus $[G : H] = [G : K]\cdot [K : H]$.

Theorem 2. If $G$ is a finite group and $H$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $|H|$ divides $|G|$.

Proposition 12. A subgroup $H$ of a group $G$ is normal in $G$ if and only if for every $a\in G$ $$aH = Ha$$

Proposition 13. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$. Then the binary operator $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \star: G / H\times G / H & \to G / H \\ (aH, bH) & \mapsto (aH)(bH) \overset{\text{def}}{=} (ab)H \end{alignat*} $$ is well-defined if and only if $H$ is normal in $G$.

Proof. Suppose that the map $\star$ is well-defined. Given $a\in H$, given $g\in G$, we have $$1 \sim_H a \text{ and } g^{-1}\sim_H g^{-1}$$ so $g^{-1}\sim_H ag^{-1}$, thus $gag^{-1}\in H$. Since $a, g$ are arbitrary, we have $H \trianglelefteq G$.

Conversely, suppose that $H$ is normal in $G$. Given $a, b, c, d\in G$ such that $a\sim_H c$ and $b\sim_H d$, we have $$(ab)^{-1}cd = b^{-1}\left(\left(a^{-1}c\right)\left(b\left(b^{-1}d\right)b^{-1}\right)\right)b \in H$$ in another words, $ab\sim cd$. Hence the map $\star$ is well-defined.

Proposition 14. Let $G$ be a group and let $H$ be a normal subgroup of $G$. Then $G / H$ is a group under the binary operator $\star$ in the preceeding proposition, called the quotient group of $G$ by $N$. The identity of this group is $H$, the inverse of an element $aH$ is $a^{-1}H$.

Suppose that $G$ is a group, $H, K$ are subgroup of $G$ such that $H$ is normal in $G$ and $H$ is a subgroup of $K$, then $H$ is normal in $K$ and $$K / H = \left\{kH : k\in K\right\} \subseteq \left\{gH : g\in G\right\} = G / H$$ so $K / H$ is a subgroup of $G / H$.

Homomorphisms ­¤öŚ

Definition 12. Let $G, H$ be groups. A homomorphism (of groups) is a map $\varphi: G\to H$ that preserves struture of group $$\varphi(ab) = \varphi(a)\varphi(b)\text{ for every }a, b\in G$$

A kernel of a homomorphism $\varphi: G\to H$ is defined by $$\ker \varphi \overset{\text{def}}{=} \left\{a\in G : \varphi(a) = 1\right\}$$

Proposition 15. Images of subgroups under a homomorphism are also subgroups. Inverse images of (normal) subgroups are also (normal) subgroups.

In particular, the image of a homomorphism is a subgroup, and its kernel is a normal subgroup.

Proposition 16. The composite of two homomorphisms is a homomorphism. The restriction of a homomorphism into a subgroup is a homomorphism. Is a homomorphism is bijective, its inverse is also a homomorphism.

Proposition 17. A subgroup of a group is normal if and only if it is the kernel of some homomorphism.

Proof. Let $H$ be a normal subgroup of a group $G$. Define the projection of $G$ into $H$ by $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \pi_H : G & \to H \\ a & \mapsto aH \end{alignat*} $$ then $\pi_H$ is a homomorphism. It kernel is $$\ker \pi_H = \left\{a\in G : aH = H\right\} = H$$

Definition 13. An isomorphism (of groups) is a bijection homomorphism. A group $G$ is said to be isomorphic to a group $H$ if there is an isomorphism from $G$ into $H$. In this case, we write $G\cong H$.

Proposition 18. A homomorphism $\varphi: G\to H$ is injective if and only if $\ker\varphi = 1$.

Theorem 3. Let $G, H$ be groups and let $\varphi: G\to H$ be a homomorphism. Then there is a unique homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: G / \ker \varphi \to H$ such that $$\varphi = \overline{\varphi}\circ \pi_{\ker \varphi}$$ where $\pi_{\ker\varphi}: G \to G / \ker\varphi$ is the projection homomorphism. Furthermore, $\overline{\varphi}$ is injective, which deduces that $$G / \ker\varphi \cong \varphi(G)$$

In particular, if $\varphi$ is surjective, then $G / \ker\varphi \cong H$.

Proof. Given $a, b\in G$ such that $a\sim_{\ker\varphi} b$, equivalently, $a^{-1}b\in \ker\varphi$, then $$\varphi(b) = \varphi(a)\varphi(a)^{-1}\varphi(b) = \varphi(a)\varphi\left(a^{-1}b\right) = \varphi(a)$$ Therefore, the map $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \overline{\varphi}: G / \ker\varphi & \to H \\ a\ker \varphi & \mapsto \varphi(a) \end{alignat*} $$ is well-defined. This map is a homomorphism $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \overline{\varphi}\left((a\ker\varphi) (b\ker\varphi)\right) & = \overline{\varphi}\left((ab)\ker\varphi\right) \\ & = \varphi(ab) \\ & = \varphi(a)\varphi(b) \\ & = \overline{\varphi}(a\ker\varphi)\overline{\varphi}(b\ker\varphi) \end{alignat*} $$ For every $a\in G$, we have $$\varphi(a) = \overline{\varphi}(a\ker\varphi) = \left(\overline{\varphi}\circ \pi_{\ker\varphi}\right)(a)$$ Thus $\varphi = \overline{\varphi}\circ \pi_{\ker\varphi}$. Suppose that $\psi: G/\ker\varphi\to H$ is a homomorphism such that $$\varphi = \psi\circ \pi_{\ker\varphi}$$ Given $a\in G$, we have $$\overline{\varphi}(a\ker\varphi) = \varphi(a) = (\psi\circ \pi_{\ker\varphi})(a) = \psi(a\ker\varphi)$$ Thus $\psi = \overline{\varphi}$, which implies the uniqueness of $\overline{\varphi}$.

The injectivity of $\varphi$ is followed by $$\ker\overline{\varphi} = \left\{a\ker\varphi\in G / \ker\varphi : \varphi(a) = 1\right\} = \{\ker\varphi\} = 1$$

Proposition 19. Let $\varphi: G\to H$ be a homomorphism, let $N$ be a normal subgroup of $G$. Then the map $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \overline{\varphi}: G / N & \to H \\ gN & \mapsto \varphi(g) \end{alignat*} $$ is well-defined if and only if $N \leqslant \ker\varphi$. If $\overline{\varphi}$ is well-defined, then $\overline{\varphi}$ is a homomorphism.

Theorem 4. Let $G$ be groups and $H, K$ be subgroups of $G$. if $K$ normalizes $H$, then $$K / (K\cap H) \cong KH / H$$

Proof. Suppose that $K$ normalizes $H$, then $HK$ is a subgroup of $G$ and $H$ is normal in $HK$. Define $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \varphi: K & \to KH / H \\ k & \mapsto kH \end{alignat*} $$ This map is clearly a homomorphism. Given $(kh)H\in KH/H$, we have $kH = (kh)H$, so $$\varphi(k) = kH = (kh)H$$ hence $\varphi$ is surjective. We have $$\ker\varphi = \left\{k\in K : kH = H\right\} = \{k\in K : k = H\} = K \cap H$$ By the first isomorphism theorem, $$K / (K\cap H) = K / \ker\varphi \cong KH / H$$

Theorem 5. Let $G$ be a group and let $H, K$ be normal subgroups of $G$ such that $H$ is a subgroup of $K$. Then $K / H$ is a normal subgroup of $G / H$ and $$(G / H) / (K / H) \cong G / K$$

Proof. Define $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \varphi: G / H & \to G / K \\ gH & \mapsto gK \end{alignat*} $$ Given $g_1, g_2\in G$ such that $g_1\sim_H g_2$, we have $g_1^{-1}g_2\in H$. Since $H\leqslant K$, we have $g_1^{-1}g_2\in K$, so $g_1\sim_K g_2$. Thus, $\varphi$ is well-defined. $\varphi$ is clearly a surjective homomorphism.

We have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \ker \varphi & = \left\{gH \in G / H : gK = K\right\} \\ & = \left\{gH\in G / H : g\in K\right\} \\ & = K / H \end{alignat*} $$ Therefore, $K / H$ is normal in $G / H$ and $$(G / H) / (K / H) \cong G / K$$

Theorem 6. Let $N$ be a normal subgroup of a group $G$. Then $A\mapsto A / N$ is a bijection between the set of all subgroups of $G$ that contains $N$ and the group $G / N$. Furthermore, for every subgroups $A, B$ of $G$ that contains $N$

  1. $A \leqslant B$ if and only if $A / N \leqslant B / N$.
  2. $A \trianglelefteq B$ if and only if $A / N \trianglelefteq B / N$.
  3. $\left<A, B\right> / N = \left<A / N, B / N\right>$.
  4. $A / N \cap B / N = (A \cap B) / N$.
  5. If $A \trianglelefteq B$ then $[B : A] = \left[B / N : A / N\right]$.

Proof. Let $\pi_N : G\to G/N$ be the projection from $G$ into $G / N$.

Suppose that $H$ is a subgroup $G / N$, let $K = \pi_N^{-1}(H) = \bigcup H$. Clearly, $K$ is a subgroup of $G$. Since $N\in H$, we have $N\subseteq K$ and $$K / N = \left\{kN : k\in K\right\} = \pi_N(K) = \pi_N\left(\pi_N^{-1}(H)\right) = H$$

Hence, the map $A\mapsto A / N$ is surjective. Suppose that $A, B$ are subgroups of $G$ containing $N$ such that $A / N = B / N$. We have $$A = \bigcup A / N = \bigcup B / N = B$$ so the map $A\mapsto A / N$ is injective. Therefore, the map $A\to A / N$ is bijective.

Given two subgroups $A, B$ of $G$ that contains $N$.

Prove 1. If $A \leqslant N$, then $$A / N = \pi_N(A) = \left(\pi_N\restriction_{B}\right)(A) \leqslant \left(\pi_N\restriction_{B}\right)(B) = \pi_N(B) = B / N$$ and if $A / N \leqslant B / N$ then $$A = \bigcup A / N \subseteq \bigcup B / N = B \implies A \leqslant B$$

Prove 2. If $A \trianglelefteq B$, then by the third isomorphism theorem, $A / N$ is normal in $B / N$. Conversely, if $A / N \trianglelefteq B / N$, then $A/ N \leqslant B / N$, so $A \leqslant B$, thus $$A = \pi_N^{-1}(A / N) = \pi_N^{-1}(A / N)\cap B = \left(\pi_N\restriction_{B}\right)^{-1}(A / N) \trianglelefteq B / N$$

Prove 3. $A, B$ are subgroups of $\left<A, B\right>$ so $A/N, B/N$ are subgroups of $\left<A, B\right> / N$. Therefore $\left<A / N, B / N\right>$ is a subgroup of $\left<A, B\right> / N$.

Since $\left<A / N, B / N\right>$ is a subgroup of $G / N$, there is a subgroup $C$ of $G$ containing $N$ such that $C / N = \left<A / N, B / N\right>$. We have $A / N$ and $B / N$ are subgroups of $C / N$, so $A, B$ are subgroups of $C$. Therefore $\left<A, B\right>$ is a subgroup of $C$, which implies that $\left<A, B\right> / N$ is a subgroup of $C / N$.

Therefore $\left<A / N, B / N\right> = \left<A, B\right> / N$.

Prove 4. There is a subgroup $C$ of $G$ containing $N$ such that $C / N = A / N \cap B / N$. We have $C / N$ is a subgroup of both $A / N$ and $B / N$, so $C$ is a subgroup of $A$ and $B$, so $C$ is a subgroup of $A \cap B$.

Conversely, $(A\cap B) / N$ is a subgroup of $A / N$ and $B / N$, so $(A \cap B) / N$ is a subgroup of $A / N \cap B / N = C / N$. Hence, $A \cap B$ is a subgroup of $C$.

Therefore $C = A\cap B$, which means that $A / N \cap B / N = (A\cap B) / N$.

Prove 5. By the third isomorphism theorem, $$B / A \cong (B / N) / (A / N) \implies [B : A] = [B / N : A / N]$$

Cyclic groups ­¤öŚ

Definition 14. A group $G$ is said to be cyclic if it can be generated by a single element, i.e., there is an element $a\in G$ such that $G = \left<a\right>$.

Proposition 20. Let $a$ be an element of a group $G$. Then elements of $\left<a\right>$ are powers of $a$.

Proposition 21. Let $G$ be a group and let $a$ be an element of $G$.

  1. If $a$ is of finite order $n$, then $a^m = 1$ if and only if $n$ divides $m$.
  2. If $a$ is infinite, then $a^n \ne a^m$ for every integers $n\ne m$.

Proof.

Prove 1. Given $b\in \left<a\right>$, there is an integer $m$ such that $b = a^m$. By Euclidean’s Property, we can write $m = qn + r$ for some integers $q\in \mathbb{Z}$ and $r\in \{0, 1, …, n - 1\}$. Thus $$b = a^{nq + r} = (a^n)^qa^r = a^r$$ It follows that any element of $\left<a\right>$ can be written in the form of $a^r$ for some non-negative integer $r \in \{0, 1, 2, …, n - 1\}$. Hence, $$\left<a\right> = \{1, a, a^2, …, a^{n - 1}\}$$ By definition, the set $\left<a\right>$ has exactly $n$ element, so the powers $1, a, a^2, …, a^{n - 1}$ are distinct.

Given $m\in \mathbb{Z}$, we can write $m = qn + r$ as above. If $a^m = 1$, then $$1 = a^{m} = a^{r}$$ so $r = 0$, therefore $m = qn$ is divisible by $n$.

Prove 2. Assume that there are two integers $n, m$ such that $n\ne m$ and $a^n = a^m$.

Given $b\in \left<a\right>$, there is an integer $k$ such that $b = a^k$. By Euclidean’s Property, we can write $$k = q|n - m| + r$$ for some integers $q\in \mathbb{Z}$ and $r\in \{0, 1, …, |n - m| - 1\}$. Thus $$b = a^{q|n - m| + r} = a^{r}$$ Hence $$\left<a\right> \subseteq \left\{1, a, a^2, …, a^{|n - m| - 1}\right\}$$ which contradicts to the infinity of $\left<a\right>$.

Therefore the assumption is wrong, in another words, $a^n \ne a^m$ for every integers $n\ne m$.

Corollary 4. Let $x$ be an element of a group $G$. Then if $x^m = x^n = 1$, then $x^d = 1$ where $d$ is the greatest common divisor of $n$ and $m$.

Theorem 7. Let $G$ be a cyclic group. Then

  1. If $G$ is finite, $G\cong \mathbb{Z} / |G|\mathbb{Z}$.
  2. If $G$ is infinite, then $G\cong \mathbb{Z}$.

Proof. Suppose that $G = \left<a\right>$ for some $a\in G$. Define $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \varphi: \mathbb{Z} & \to G \\ n & \mapsto a^n \end{alignat*} $$ Then $\varphi$ is a surjective homomorphism.

In the case that $G$ is finite, we have $$\ker\varphi = \{n\in \mathbb{Z} : a^{n} = 1\} = \{n\in \mathbb{Z} : |G| \text{ divides }n\} = |G|\mathbb{Z}$$ By the first isomorphism theorem, $\mathbb{Z} / |G|\mathbb{Z} \cong G$.

In the case that $|G|$ is infinite, we have $$\ker\varphi = \{n\in \mathbb{Z} : a^n = 1\} = \{0\}$$ so $\varphi$ is injective. By the first isomorphism theore, $G\cong \mathbb{Z}$.

Proposition 22. Let $G = \left<a\right>$ be a cyclic group. Then

  1. If $|a|$ is infinite, then $\left|a^n\right|$ is infinite for every integer $n\ne 0$.
  2. If $|a| = m$ is finite, then $\left|a^n\right| = \dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}$ for every integer $n$.

Proof.

The map $\varphi: \mathbb{Z} \ni k\mapsto a^{nk}\in \left<a^n\right>$ is a surjective homomorphism from $\mathbb{Z}$ into $\left<a^n\right>$.

Prove 1. For every $k\in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $a^{nk} = 1$ if and only if $nk = 0$, so $k = 0$ as $n\ne 0$. Thus $$\ker\varphi = \{k\in \mathbb{Z} : a^{nk} = 1\} = \{0\}$$ which means $\varphi$ is injective, and so a isomorphism.

Prove 2. For every $k\in \mathbb{Z}$, we have $a^{nk} = 1$ if and only if $nk$ is divisible by $m$, equivalently, $k$ is divisible by $\dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}$. Hence $$\ker \varphi = \{k\in \mathbb{Z} : a^{nk} = 1\} = \left\{k\in \mathbb{Z} : \dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)} \mid k\right\} = \dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}\mathbb{Z}$$ By the first isomorphism theore, $\left<a^n\right> \cong \mathbb{Z} / \dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}\mathbb{Z}$. Therefore, $\left|a^n\right| = \dfrac{m}{\gcd(n, m)}$.

Corollary 5. Let $G$ be a cyclic group of finite order $n$, let $k$ be an integer relatively prime to $n$. Then the map $a\mapsto a^k$ is an isomorphism from $G$ into itself.

Theorem 8. Let $G = \left<a\right>$ be a cyclic group. Then

  1. Every subgroup of $G$ is a cyclic group generated by some power of $a$.
  2. If $G$ is infinite, then subgroups of $G$ are $\left\{1\right\}, \left<a\right>, \left<a^2\right>, …$ and these subgroups are distinct.
  3. If $G$ is of finite order $n$, then for every integer $m$, $\left<a^m\right> = \left<a^{\gcd(n, m)}\right>$. So that for every positive divisor $d$ of $n$, $\left<a^{n / d}\right>$ is the unique subgroup of order $d$ of $G$.

Proof. Given a subgroup $H$ of $G$, suppose that $H \ne \{1\}$. By the well-ordering property, choose $m$ the smallest positive integer such that $a^m\in H$.

Given $b\in H$, there is an integer $k$ such that $b = a^k$. By Euclidean’s Property, we can write $$b = qm + r$$ for some integers $q\in \mathbb{Z}$ and $r\in \{0, 1, 2, …, m - 1\}$. Then $a^r = a^{-qm}b\in H$.

By the minimality of $m$, $a^r = 1$, so $b = a^{qm} = (a^m)^q \in \left<a^m\right>$.

Therefore, $H = \left<a^m\right>$.

Proposition 23. If $G$ is a group such that $G/Z(G)$ is cyclic, then $G$ is abelian.

Proposition 24. Let $G$ be a group, let $x, y$ be elements of $G$. Suppose that $|x|$ and $|y|$ are finite and relatively prime, and suppose that $xy = yx$. Then $\left<x\right>\left<y\right> = \left<xy\right>$ and $|xy| = |x|\cdot|y|$.
