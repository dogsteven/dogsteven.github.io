Linear Algebra: Matrices and Algebraic Duals

Assume that all vector spaces in this post are vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{K}$.

Coordinate, matrices 🔗

Definition 1. Let $V$ be a vector space of dimension $n$, let $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n)$ be an ordered basis for $V$. For each vector $\mathbf{v}\in V$, we can write $\mathbf{v}$ uniquely as $$\mathbf{v} = a_1\cdot \alpha_1 + a_2\cdot \alpha_2 + … + a_n\cdot \alpha_n$$ Then the tuple $(a_1, a_2, …, a_n) \in \mathbb{K}^n$ is called the coordinate of $\mathbf{v}$ with respect to the ordered basis $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n)$. If $\mathcal{A}$ is an ordered basis for $V$, the coordinate of a vector $\mathbf{v}$ with respect to $\mathcal{A}$ is denoted by $\left(\mathbf{v}\right)_{\mathcal{A}}$. The map $$\left(\cdot\right)_{\mathcal{A}}: V\to \mathbb{K}^n$$ is a linear isomorphism.

Definition 2. A matrix (over a field $\mathbb{K}$) of dimension $n\times m$ is a collection $\left(a_{i,j}\right)$ of scalars for $i \in \{1, 2, …, n\}$ and $j\in \{1, 2, …, m\}$. We can visualize a matrix as a rectangular of size $n\times m$ $$ \left[ \begin{matrix} a_{1,1} & a_{1,2} & \cdots & a_{1, m} \\ a_{2,1} & a_{2,2} & \cdots & a_{2, m} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{n,1} & a_{n,2} & \cdots & a_{n, m} \\ \end{matrix} \right] $$ The set of all matrices of dimension $n\times m$ is denoted by $\text{Mat}_{n\times m}(\mathbb{K})$. If $A$ is a matrix of size $n\times m$, we denote the value of cell at row $i$, column $j$ by $A_{i,j}$.

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces of dimension $n$ and $m$, respectively. Let $\varphi: V\to W$ be a linear homomorphism. Let $\mathcal{A} = (\alpha_1, …, \alpha_n)$ and $\mathcal{B} = (\beta_1, …, \beta_m)$ be ordered bases of $V$ and $W$, respectively. Suppose that $$(\varphi(\alpha_j))_{\mathcal{B}} = (a_{1,j}, a_{2,j}, …, a_{m,j})$$ for each $j\in \{1, 2, …, n\}$. Since $\varphi$ is uniquely determined by values on a basis, it follows that with respect to $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, $\varphi$ is uniquely determined by the value of the matrix $(a_{i, j})$.

Theorem 1. There is a bijection from $\mathcal{L}(V, W)$ into $\text{Mat}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ defined as above. This bijection induces a vector space structure on $\text{Mat}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ where

  1. $(a_{i, j}) + (b_{i, j}) = (a_{i, j} + b_{i, j})$,
  2. $k\cdot (a_{i, j}) = (ka_{i, j})$

With respect to this structure, the bijection becomes a linear isomorphism. We denote this linear isomorphism by $\left[\cdot\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{B}}: \mathcal{L}(V, W)\to \text{Mat}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{K})$.

$\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{B}}$ is called the matrix $\varphi$ with respect to bases $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B})$.

Let $U$ be a vector space of dimension $p$ with an ordered basis $\mathcal{C}$.

Let $\psi: W\to U$ be a linear homomorphism, suppose that $$\left(\psi(\beta_k)\right)_{\mathcal{C}} = (b_{1,k}, b_{2,k}, …, b_{p,k})$$ for every $k\in \{1, 2, …, m\}$. Then $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \left(\psi\circ \varphi(\alpha_j)\right)_{\mathcal{C}} & = \left(\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m}a_{k,j}\cdot \psi(\beta_k)\right)_{\mathcal{C}} \\ & = \sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} a_{k,j}\cdot (b_{1,k}, b_{2,k}, …, b_{p,k}) \\ & = \left(\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} b_{1, k}a_{k,j}, \sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} b_{2, k}a_{k,j}, …, \sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} b_{p, k}a_{k,j}\right) \end{alignat*} $$ Hence $$\left[\psi\circ \varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{C}} = (c_{i,j}) = \left(\sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} b_{i,k}a_{k,j}\right)$$

Definition 3. The matrix multiplication $\cdot : \text{Mat}_{p\times m}(\mathbb{K})\times \text{Mat}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{K}) \to \text{Mat}_{p\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ is defined as follows: $$(A\cdot B)_{i,j} = \sum\limits_{k = 1}^{m} A_{i,k}B_{k,j}$$

Theorem 2. For every $\varphi\in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$ and $\psi\in \mathcal{L}(W, U)$, we have $$\left[\psi\circ \varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{C}} = \left[\psi\right]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{C}}\cdot \left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{B}}$$

Theorem 3. The map $\varphi: V \to \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{K}, V)$ carrying each vector $\mathbf{v}$ into the linear homomorphism $\mathbf{v}: \mathbb{K}\to V$ $$\mathbf{v}(a) = a\cdot \mathbf{v}$$ For each ordered basis $\mathcal{A}$ of $V$, we write $\left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{A}}$ as an abbreviation $\left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{(1)}^{\mathcal{A}}$

Since $\mathbf{v}(1) = \mathbf{v}$, the matrix $\left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{A}}$ is just the coordinate $(\mathbf{v})_{\mathcal{A}}$ written in column vector.

For every linear homomorphism $\psi: V\to W$, we have $$\left[\psi\circ \mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{B}} = \left[\psi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{B}}\cdot \left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{A}}$$ By a similar argument, $\left[\psi\circ \mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{B}}$ is just the coordinate $(\psi(\mathbf{v}))_{\mathcal{B}}$ written in column vector.

Endomorphisms, change of basis 🔗

Definition 4. Let $V$ be a vector space, an endomorphism on $V$ is a linear homomorphism from $V$ into itself. We denote the vector space of all endomorphisms on $V$ by $\text{End}(V)$.

Theorem 4. Let $V$ be a vector space of dimension $n$. $\text{End}(V)$ is a ring with unity under addition and composition. The linear isomorphism $[\cdot]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}}: \text{End}(V)\to \text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ makes $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ into a ring with unity under matrix addition and matrix multiplication. Under this structure, $[\cdot]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}}$ becomes a ring homomorphism. The identity of $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ is $$E_n = \left[\begin{matrix}1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{matrix}\right]$$

Definition 5. A matrix $A$ in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ is said to be invertible if there is a matrix $B$ in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ such that $$A\cdot B = B\cdot A = E_n$$

Proposition 1. A matrix $A$ in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ is said to be invertible if and only if there is a matrix $B$ in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ such that $A\cdot B = E_n$ or $B\cdot A = E_n$.

Proposition 2. A matrix $A$ in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ is said to be invertible if and only if given a vector space $V$ of dimension $n$, there exists a linear isomorphism $\varphi\in \text{End}(V)$ such that $\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}} = A$ for every ordered basis $\mathcal{A}$ of $V$. Furthermore, $\left[\varphi^{-1}\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}} = A^{-1}$.

Corollary 1. Let $A$ and $B$ be invertible matrices in $\text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$, then $A\cdot B$ is invertible and $$(A\cdot B)^{-1} = B^{-1}\cdot A^{-1}$$

Definition 6. Let $V$ be a vector space of dimension $n$, let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be two ordered bases of $V$. The matrix $C$ of the identity map with respect to bases $(\mathcal{B}, \mathcal{A})$ is called the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$. We have $$\left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{A}} = C\cdot \left[\mathbf{v}\right]_{\mathcal{B}}$$ for every $\mathbf{v}\in V$.

Proposition 3. If $C$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$, then $C$ is invertible and $C^{-1}$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{A}$.

Theorem 5. Let $V$ be a finite dimensional vector space, let $\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2$ be ordered bases of $V$. Let $W$ be a vector space with ordered bases $\mathcal{B}_1$ and $\mathcal{B}_2$. Let $A$ and $B$ be the change-of-basis matrices from $\mathcal{A}_1$ to $\mathcal{A}_2$ and from $\mathcal{B}_1$ to $\mathcal{B}_2$, respectively. Then for every linear homomorphism $\varphi\in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$, we have $$\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}_1}^{\mathcal{B}_1} = B^{-1}\cdot \left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}_2}^{\mathcal{B}_2}\cdot A$$

Corollary 2. Let $C$ be the change-of-basis from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$, for every endomorphism $\varphi$, we have $$\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}} = C^{-1}\cdot \left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{B}}\cdot C$$

For short, we write $\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}$ as an abbreviation of $\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}}$, and call it the matrix of $\varphi$ with respect to the ordered basis $\mathcal{A}$.

Proposition 4. If $C$ is an invertible matrix and $\mathcal{A}$ is an ordered basis, then there is an ordered basis $\mathcal{B}$ such that $C$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$.

Proof. There is a linear isomorphism $\varphi: V\to V$ whose matrix with respect to $\mathcal{A}$ is $C^{-1}$. Consider $$\mathcal{B} = (\beta_1, \beta_2, …, \beta_n) = \left(\varphi(\alpha_1), \varphi(\alpha_2), …, \varphi(\alpha_n)\right)$$ where $\mathcal{A} = (\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n)$. By definition, $$\left[\varphi^{-1}\right]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{A}} = E_n$$ so $$[\mathbf{i}_V]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{A}} = \left[\varphi\circ \varphi^{-1}\right]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{A}} = \left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}}\cdot \left[\varphi^{-1}\right]_{\mathcal{B}}^{\mathcal{A}} = C\cdot E_n = C$$

Definition 7. Two matrices $A, B\in \text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ are called similar if there is an invertible matrix $P$ of dimension $n\times n$ such that $$A = P^{-1}\cdot B\cdot P$$

Theorem 6. Two matrices $A, B\in \text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ are similar if and only if given a vector space $V$ of dimension $n$, there is an endomorphism $\varphi$ on $V$ such that $$\left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{A}} = A \text{ and } \left[\varphi\right]_{\mathcal{B}} = B$$ for some ordered bases $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ for $V$.

Proof. Suppose that $A, B\in \text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ are similar, i.e., $A = P^{-1}\cdot B\cdot P$ for some invertible matrix $P$.

Given a metric space $V$ of dimension $n$, given an ordered basis $\mathcal{A}$ for $V$. Since $\left[\cdot\right]_{\mathcal{A}}^{\mathcal{A}}$ is a linear isomorphism, so there is an endomorphism $\varphi: V\to V$ whose matrix with respect to $\mathcal{A}$ is $A$. Since $P$ is invertible, there is an ordered basis $\mathcal{B}$ of $V$ such that $P$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $\mathcal{A}$ to $\mathcal{B}$. Then $$[\varphi]_{\mathcal{B}} = P\cdot [\varphi]_{\mathcal{A}}\cdot P^{-1} = P\cdot A\cdot P^{-1} = B$$

The algebraic duals 🔗

Definition 8. Let $V$ be a vector space. The algebraic dual of $V$ is defined to be $\mathcal{L}(V, \mathbb{K})$ and it’s denoted by $V^{\star}$.

Definition 9. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a basis for a vector space $V$. For each $\alpha\in V$, let $\alpha^{\star}: V\to \mathbb{K}$ be the linear homomorphism such that $$\alpha^{\star}(\alpha) = 1 \text{ and } \alpha^{\star}(\beta) = 0 \text{ for every }\beta\in \mathcal{A}\setminus \{\alpha\}$$ This definition determines a map from $\mathcal{A}$ into $V^{\star}$, which can be extended into a linear homomorphism from $V$ into $V^{\star}$. We denote this linear homomorphism by $$(\cdot)^{\star}: V\ni \mathbf{v}\mapsto \mathbf{v}^{\star}\in V^{\star}$$ Furthermore, $\mathbf{v}^{\star}(\alpha) = \alpha^{\star}(\mathbf{v})$ for every $\mathbf{v}\in V$ and $\alpha\in \mathcal{A}$. The vector $\mathbf{v}^{\star}$ is called the dual vector of $\mathbf{v}$ with respect to $\mathcal{A}$.

Proposition 5. Given a basis $\mathcal{A}$ for a vector space $V$ and define the operator $(\cdot)^{\star}$ as above. Then for every $\mathbf{v}\in V$ $$\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \text{ if and only if }\alpha^{\star}(\mathbf{v}) = 0\text{ for every }\alpha\in \mathcal{A}$$ As a consequence, we have the following theorem:

Proposition 6. Given a basis $\{\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n\}$ for a vector space $V$. Then for every $\mathcal{v}\in V$, $$\mathbf{v}^{\star} = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} \alpha_i^{\star}(\mathbf{v})\cdot \alpha_i^{\star} = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} \mathbf{v}^{\star}(\alpha_i)\cdot \alpha_i^{\star}$$

Theorem 7. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a basis for a vector space $V$. Then the linear homomorphism carrying each $\alpha\in \mathcal{A}$ into $\alpha^{\star}$ is injective.

If $V$ is finite dimensional, this linear homomorphism is a linear isomorphism. In this case, $\{\alpha_1^{\star}, \alpha_2^{\star}, …, \alpha_n^{\star}\}$ is a basis for $V^{\star}$, called the dual basis with respect to $\mathcal{A}$.

In fact, if $\mathcal{A}$ is a basis for $V$, then $$V^{\star} \cong \left(\bigoplus\limits_{\alpha\in \mathcal{A}}\mathbb{K}\right)^{\star} \cong \prod\limits_{\alpha\in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{K}^{\star} \cong \prod\limits_{\alpha\in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{K} = \mathbb{K}^{|\mathcal{A}|}$$

Definition 10. Let $V, W$ be vector spaces. Given a a linear homomorphism $\varphi: V\to W$, define the dual $\varphi^{\star}: W^{\star}\to V^{\star}$ of $\varphi$ by $$\varphi^{\star}(\psi) = \psi\circ \varphi$$

Theorem 8. The map $(\cdot)^{\star}: \mathcal{L}(V, W)\ni \varphi\mapsto \varphi^{\star}\in \mathcal{L}(W^{\star}, V^{\star})$ is an injective linear homomorphism. If $V$ and $W$ are finite dimensional, it becomes a linear isomorphism.

Proposition 7. Let $V, W, U$ be vector spaces. Rhen for every $\varphi\in \mathcal{L}(V, W)$ and $\psi\in \mathcal{L}(W, U)$, $$(\psi\circ \varphi)^{\star} = \varphi^{\star}\circ \psi^{\star}$$

Corollary 3. If $\varphi: V\to W$ is a linear isomorphism, then $$\left(\varphi^{-1}\right)^{\star} = \left(\varphi^{\star}\right)^{-1}$$

Theorem 9. Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces and let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be ordered bases for $V$ and $W$, respectively. Suppose that $V$ is of dimension $n$ and $W$ is of dimension $m$. Let $\mathcal{A}^{\star}$ and $\mathcal{B}^{\star}$ be the ordered dual bases with respect to $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$, respectively. Given a linear homomorphism $\varphi: V\to W$, let $A\in \text{Mat}_{m\times n}(\mathbb{K})$ be the matrix of $\varphi$ with respect to $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{B})$. Then the matrix of $\varphi^{\star}$ with respect to $(\mathcal{B}^{\star}, \mathcal{A}^{\star})$ is the matrix $A^T\in \text{Mat}_{n\times m}(\mathbb{K})$ where $$A^T_{i, j} = A_{j, i}$$ for every $i\in \{1, 2, …, n\}$ and $j\in \{1, 2, …, m\}$. The matrix $A^T$ is called the transpose of $A$.

Proof. Suppose that $\mathcal{A} = (\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n)$ and $\mathcal{B} = (\beta_1, \beta_2, …, \beta_m)$. We have $$\varphi^{\star}(\beta_i^{\star}) = \beta_i^{\star}\circ \varphi = \sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n} (\beta_i^{\star}\circ \varphi)(\alpha_j)\cdot \alpha_j^{\star} = \sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n} \beta_i^{\star}(\varphi(\alpha_j))\cdot \alpha_j = \sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n} A_{i, j}\cdot \alpha_j$$

Corollary 4. For every $A\in \text{Mat}_{n\times m}(\mathbb{K})$ and $B\in \text{Mat}_{m\times p}(\mathbb{K})$, $$B^T\cdot A^T = (A\cdot B)^T$$

Corollary 5. For every invertible matrix $A\in \text{Mat}_{n\times n}(\mathbb{K})$, we have $A^T$ is invertible and $$\left(A^{-1}\right)^T = \left(A^T\right)^{-1}$$

Definition 11. Define $V^{\star\star} = (V^{\star})^{\star}$, called it the second algebraic dual of $V$. Define $$(\cdot)^{\star\star}: V\to V^{\star\star},\;\;\mathbf{v}\mapsto \mathbf{v}^{\star\star} = (\varphi \mapsto \varphi(\mathbf{v}))$$

Proposition 8. The map $\mathbf{v}\mapsto \mathbf{v}^{\star\star}$ is an injective linear homomorphism. If $V$ is finite dimensional, it becomes a linear isomorphism.

Proposition 9. Suppose that $V$ is finite dimensional with basis $\mathcal{A}$. Then for every $\alpha\in \mathcal{A}$ $$(\alpha^{\star})^{\star} = \alpha^{\star\star}$$

Definition 12. Let $V$ be a vector space and let $W$ be a subspace of $V$. Define $$W^{\perp} = \left\{\varphi\in V^{\star} : W \subseteq \ker \varphi\right\}$$ called the orthogonal complement of $W$ in $V^{\star}$.

Theorem 10. Let $V$ be a vector space and let $W$ be a subspace of $V$, then $$(V / W)^{\star} \cong W^{\perp}\text{ and }V^{\star} / W^{\perp} \cong W^{\star}$$

Proposition 10. Let $W, U$ be subspaces of a vector space $V$, then $$(W + U)^{\perp} = W^{\perp}\cap U^{\perp}\text{ and }(W\cap U)^{\perp} = W^{\perp} + U^{\perp}$$

Theorem 11. If $\varphi: V\to W$ is a linear homomorphism, then $$\left(\varphi(V)\right)^{\perp} = \ker \varphi^{\star}\text{ and } (\ker \varphi)^{\perp} = \varphi^{\star}(W^{\star})$$
