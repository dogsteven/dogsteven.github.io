Linear Algebra: Tensor Product

Jul 10, 2022 · 1839 words · 9 minute read

Assume that all vector spaces in this post are over $\mathbb{K}$.

Definition 1. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a set. The vector space over $\mathbb{K}$ generated by $\mathcal{A}$ is defined to be the set $F(\mathcal{A})$ of all maps $\varphi: \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{K}$ such that $\varphi(\alpha) = 0$ for all but only a finite number of $\alpha$ in $\mathcal{A}$. We could see that $$F(\mathcal{A})\cong \bigoplus\limits_{\alpha\in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{K}$$ One could treat $\mathcal{A}$ as a basis for $F(\mathcal{A})$ under the embedding $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} j: \mathcal{A} & \to F(\mathcal{A}) \\ \alpha & \mapsto \mathbf{e}_{\alpha} \end{alignat*} $$ where $\mathbf{e}_{\alpha}(\beta) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if }\beta = \alpha \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$ for each $\alpha\in \mathcal{A}$.

Thus, each element of $F(\mathcal{A})$ can be written as the form $$a_1\cdot \alpha_1 + a_2 \cdot \alpha_n + … + a_n \cdot \alpha_n$$ for some $n$, scalar $a_1, a_2, …, a_n$ and $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, …, \alpha_n\in \mathcal{A}$.

Theorem 1. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a set. For every vector space $V$, for every map $\varphi: \mathcal{A}\to V$, there is a unique linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: F(\mathcal{A})\to V$ such that $$\overline{\varphi}\restriction_{\mathcal{A}} = \varphi$$

Definition 2. Let $V, U, W$ be vector spaces. A map $$\varphi: V\times U \to W$$ is called a bilinear map if $\varphi$ is linear separately in each variable, i.e., $$ \varphi(\mathbf{v}_1, + \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{u}) + \varphi(\mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{u}) \\ \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}_1 + \mathbf{u}_2) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}_1) + \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}_2) \\ \varphi(a\cdot \mathbf{v}, b\cdot \mathbf{u}) = ab\cdot \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}) $$ The set $\mathcal{B}(V\times U, W)$ of all bilinear maps from $V\times U$ into $W$ is a vector spaces under the following operations

  1. $(\varphi + \psi)(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}) + \psi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})$,
  2. $(a\cdot\varphi)(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}) = a\cdot \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})$.

Definition 3. Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces. Define $V\otimes W$ to be the quotient space $F(V\times W) / H$ where $H$ is the subspace of $F(V\times H)$ generated by vectors of the following forms

  1. $(\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w}) - (\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{w}) - (\mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w})$,
  2. $(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2) - (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1) - (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_2)$,
  3. $(a\cdot \mathbf{v}, b\cdot \mathbf{w}) - ab\cdot (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$.

Let $\otimes: V\times W\to V\otimes W$ be the map carrying $(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$ into its coset $\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}$. By definition $$(a_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_1 + a_2\cdot \mathbf{v}_2)\otimes \mathbf{w} = a_1\cdot (\mathbf{v}_1\otimes \mathbf{w}) + a_2\cdot (\mathbf{v}_2\otimes \mathbf{w})$$ and $$\mathbf{v}\otimes (a_1\cdot\mathbf{w}_1 + a_2\cdot \mathbf{w}_2) = a_1\cdot (\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}_1) + a_2\cdot (\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}_2)$$

Proposition 1. The map $\otimes: V\times W\to V\otimes W$ is a bilinear map.

Proposition 2. Let $V, U, W$ be vector spaces. For every bilinear map $\varphi: V\times U\to W$, there exists a linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: V\otimes U\to W$ such that $$\varphi = \overline{\varphi}\circ \otimes$$ More explicitly, $$\overline{\varphi}(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})$$ for every $\mathbf{v}\in V, \mathbf{u}\in U$.

Proof. Since $V\times U$ is a basis for $F(V\times U)$, $\varphi$ can be extended into a linear homomorphism $$\psi: F(V\times U)\to W$$ We have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \psi((\mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w}) - (\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{w}) - (\mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w})) & = \psi((\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w})) - \psi((\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{w})) - \psi((\mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w})) \\ & = \varphi(\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w}) - \varphi(\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{w}) - \varphi(\mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{w}) \\ & = \mathbf{0} \\ \psi((\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2) - (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1) - (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_2)) & = \psi((\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2)) - \psi((\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1)) - \psi((\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_2)) \\ & = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2) - \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_1) - \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}_2) \\ & = \mathbf{0} \\ \psi((a\cdot \mathbf{v}, b\cdot \mathbf{w}) - ab\cdot (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})) & = \psi((a\cdot \mathbf{v}, b\cdot \mathbf{w})) - ab\cdot \psi((\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})) \\ & = \varphi(a\cdot \mathbf{v}, b\cdot \mathbf{w}) - ab\cdot \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}) \\ & = \mathbf{0} \end{alignat*} $$ Hence there is a linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: V\otimes U\to W$ such that $\overline{\varphi}\circ \otimes = \psi\restriction_{V\times W} = \varphi$.

Corollary 1. Suppose that $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, …, \mathbf{v}_n\}$ is independent in $V$, then $$\sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n} \mathbf{v}_i \otimes \mathbf{w}_i = \mathbf{0}$$ implies that $\mathbf{w}_1 = \mathbf{w}_2 = … = \mathbf{w}_n = \mathbf{0}$.

Proof. The set $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, …, \mathbf{v}_n\}$ can be extended into a basis $\mathcal{A}$ for $V$.

For each $i\in \{1, 2, …, n\}$, let $\mathbf{v}_i^{\star}$ is the dual vector of $\mathbf{v}_i$ with respect to $\mathcal{A}$.

Consider the map $\varphi_i: V\times W\to W$ carrying $(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$ into $\mathbf{v}_i^{\star}(\mathbf{v})\cdot \mathbf{w}$. It’s easy to see that $\varphi_i$ is a bilinear map, so there is a linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}_i: V\otimes W\to W$ such that $$\overline{\varphi}_i(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}) = \varphi_i(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$$ for every $\mathbf{v}\in V$ and $\mathbf{w}\in W$. Hence for every $i\in \{1, 2, …, n\}$ $$\mathbf{0} = \overline{\varphi}_i\left(\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n} \mathbf{v}_j \otimes \mathbf{w}_j\right) = \sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n} \mathbf{v}_i^{\star}(\mathbf{v}_j)\cdot \mathbf{w}_j = \mathbf{w}_i$$

Theorem 2. Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces, let $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be bases for $V$ and $W$, respectively. Then the set $$\otimes(\mathcal{A}\times \mathcal{B}) = \left\{\alpha\otimes \beta : \alpha\in \mathcal{A}, \beta\in \mathcal{B}\right\}$$ is a basis for $V\otimes W$.

Theorem 3. Let $V, U, W$ be vector spaces. For every bilinear map $\varphi: V\times U\to W$, there exists a unique linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: V\otimes U\to W$ such that $$\varphi = \overline{\varphi}\circ \otimes$$ More explicitly, $$\overline{\varphi}(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})$$ for every $\mathbf{v}\in V, \mathbf{u}\in U$.

Corollary 2. For every vector space $V$, we have $\mathbb{K}\otimes V\cong V$.

Corollary 3. For every vector spaces $V, U, W$, we have $$\mathcal{B}(V\times U, W) \cong \mathcal{L}(V\otimes U, W)$$

Proposition 3. For every vector spaces $V, U, W$, we have $$\mathcal{L}(U, \mathcal{L}(V, W)) \cong \mathcal{B}(U\times V, W)\cong \mathcal{B}(V\times U, W) \cong \mathcal{L}(V, \mathcal{L}(U, W))$$

Corollary 4. For every vector spaces $V, U, W$, we have $$\mathcal{L}(V\otimes U, W) \cong \mathcal{L}(V, \mathcal{L}(U, W))\cong \mathcal{L}(U, \mathcal{L}(V, W))$$

Definition 4. Let $V_1, V_2, …, V_n, W$ be vector spaces. A map $$\varphi: V_1\times V_2\times …\times V_n\to W$$ is called a multilinear map if $\varphi$ is linear separately in each variable.

Definition 5. Let $V_1, V_2, …, V_n$ be vector spaces. A vector space $V$ togerther with a multilinear map $\otimes: V_1\times V_2\times …\times V_n \to V$ is called a tensor product of $V_1, V_2, …, V_n$ if for every vector space $W$, for every multilinear map $$\varphi: V_1\times V_2\times …\times V_n\to W$$ there is a unique linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}: V\to W$ such that $\overline{\varphi}\circ \otimes = \varphi$.

We can see that $\left(V, W, \otimes: V\times W\to V\otimes W\right)$ is a tensor product of $V, W$.

Theorem 4. Tensor product is unique up to linear isomorphism.

Proof. Suppose that $(V, \otimes_V), (Z, \otimes_Z)$ are two tensor products of $V_1, V_2, …, V_n$.

Since $\otimes_Z$ is multilinear, there is a unique linear homomorphism $\varphi: V\to Z$ such that $$\varphi\circ \otimes_V = \otimes_Z$$ Similarly, there is a unique linear homomorphism $\psi: Z\to V$ such that $$\psi\circ \otimes_Z = \otimes_V$$ Then $$\otimes_Z = \varphi\circ \otimes_V = \varphi\circ \psi\circ \otimes_Z$$ and $$\otimes_V = \psi\circ \otimes_Z = \psi\circ \varphi\circ \otimes_V$$

Since $\otimes_V$ is a multilinear, there is a unique linear homomorphism $j: V\to V$ such that $$\otimes_V = j\circ \otimes_V$$ The identity map $\mathbf{id}_V$ on $V$ satisfies the condition $\otimes_V = \mathbf{id}_V\circ \otimes_V$. By the uniqueness, $$\psi\circ \varphi = \mathbf{id}_V$$ Similarly, $\varphi\circ \psi = \mathbf{id}_Z$, which proves that $\varphi$ is a linear isomorphism.

Theorem 5. $(V\otimes U)\otimes W$ is a tensor product of $V, U, W$.

Proof. The map $\otimes: V\times U\times W\to (V\otimes U)\otimes W$ carrying $(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w})$ into $(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) \otimes \mathbf{w}$ is clearly multilinear.

Given a multilinear map $\varphi: V\times U\times W\to Z$. For each $\mathbf{w}\in W$, the map $$\varphi_{\mathbf{w}}: V\times U \to Z, \;\; (\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})\mapsto \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w})$$ is a multilinear map. There is a unique (for each $\mathbf{w}$) linear homomorphism $$\overline{\varphi}_{\mathbf{w}}: V\otimes U\to Z, \;\; \overline{\varphi}_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w})$$ Furthermore, the map $\mathbf{w}\mapsto \overline{\varphi}_{\mathbf{w}}$ is a linear homomorphism. So the map $$\psi: (V\otimes U)\times W\to Z, \;\; (\mathbf{t}, \mathbf{w}) \mapsto \overline{\varphi}_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{t})$$ is multilinear. Therefore, there is a unique map $\overline{\psi}: (V\otimes U)\otimes W\to Z$ such that $$\overline{\psi}(\mathbf{t}\otimes \mathbf{w}) = \psi(\mathbf{t}, \mathbf{w})$$ Hence $$\overline{\psi}((\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) \otimes \mathbf{w}) = \psi(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}) = \overline{\varphi}_{\mathbf{w}}(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{u}) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w})$$

Similarly, $V\otimes (U\otimes W)$ is also a tensor product of $V, U, W$, which leads us to the following theorem.

Theorem 6. $(V\otimes U)\otimes W \cong V\otimes (U\otimes W)$.

Theorem 7. For every vector spaces $V, U$, we have $$V\otimes U\cong U\otimes V$$

Corollary 5. Let $V_1, V_2, …, V_n, W$ be vector spaces. Then $$\mathcal{B}(V_1\times V_2 \times … \times V_n, W) \cong \mathcal{L}(V_1, \mathcal{B}(V_2 \times … \times V_n, W))$$

Proof. Let $V$ be a tensor product of $V_2, …, V_n$, then $V_1\otimes V$ is a tensor product of $V_1, …, V_n$. Then we have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \mathcal{B}(V_1\times V_2 \times … \times V_n, W) & \cong \mathcal{L}(V_1\otimes V, W) \\ & \cong \mathcal{L}(V_1, \mathcal{L}(V, W)) \\ & \cong \mathcal{L}(V_1, \mathcal{B}(V_2 \times … \times V_n, W)) \end{alignat*} $$

Corollary 6. Let $V_1, V_2, …, V_n, W$ be vector spaces. Then $$\mathcal{B}(V_1\times V_2 \times … \times V_n, W) \cong \mathcal{B}(V_2 \times … \times V_n, \mathcal{L}(V_1, W))$$

Proof. Let $V$ be a tensor product of $V_2, …, V_n$, then $V_1\otimes V$ is a tensor product of $V_1, …, V_n$. Then we have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \mathcal{B}(V_1\times V_2 \times … \times V_n, W) & \cong \mathcal{L}(V_1\otimes V, W) \\ & \cong \mathcal{L}(V, \mathcal{L}(V_1, W)) \\ & \cong \mathcal{B}(V_2 \times … \times V_n, \mathcal{L}(V_1, W)) \end{alignat*} $$

Definition 6. Let $V_1, V_2, W_1, W_2$ be vector spaces, let $\varphi: V_1\to V_2, \psi: W_1\to W_2$ be linear homomorphisms. The map $\otimes \circ (\varphi\times \psi): V_1\times V_2 \to W_1\otimes W_2$ is a multilinear map, so it induces a unique linear homomorphism $$\varphi\otimes \psi: V_1\otimes V_2\to W_1\otimes W_2$$ having the property $(\varphi\otimes \psi)(\mathbf{v}_1\otimes \mathbf{v}_2) = \varphi(\mathbf{v}_1)\otimes \psi(\mathbf{v}_2)$. This map is called the tensor product of $\varphi$ and $\psi$.

Proposition 4. For every $\varphi, \varphi_1, \varphi_2\in \mathcal{L}(V_1, V_2)$ and $\psi, \psi_1, \psi_2\in \mathcal{L}(W_1, W_2)$, $$\varphi\otimes (\psi_1 + \psi_2) = \varphi\otimes \psi_1 + \varphi\otimes \psi_2, \text{ and }(\varphi_1 + \varphi_2)\otimes \psi = \varphi_2\otimes \psi + \varphi_2\otimes \psi$$ Also $$\varphi\otimes \psi = (\mathbf{id}_{V_2}\otimes \psi) \circ (\varphi\otimes \mathbf{id}_{W_1}) = (\varphi\otimes \mathbf{id}_{W_2}) \circ (\mathbf{id}_{V_1}\otimes \psi)$$

Theorem 8. Let $\{V_i\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}$ be a collection of vector spaces, let $W$ be a vector space. Then $$\left(\bigoplus\limits_{i\in \mathcal{I}} V_i\right)\otimes W \cong \bigoplus\limits_{i\in \mathcal{I}} \left(V_i\otimes W\right)$$

Proof. Let $\left(V, \{\mathbf{i}_i : V_i\to V\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}\right)$ be a direct sum of $\{V_i\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}$. For each $i\in \mathcal{I}$, define $$\mathbf{j}_i = \mathbf{i}_i \otimes \mathbf{id}_W: V_i\otimes W \to V\otimes W$$

Given a collection $\{\varphi_i: V_i\otimes W\to Z\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}$ of linear homomorphisms.

For each $i\in \mathcal{I}$, there is a unique linear homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}_i: V_i\to \mathcal{L}(W, Z)$ such that $$\overline{\varphi}_i(\mathbf{v})(\mathbf{w}) = \varphi_i(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w})$$

So there is a unique linear homomorphism $\psi: V \to \mathcal{L}(W, Z)$ such that for every $i\in \mathcal{I}$ $$\psi\circ \mathbf{i}_i = \overline{\varphi}_i$$

Thus, there is a unique linear homomorphism $\overline{\psi}: V\otimes W\to Z$ such that $$\overline{\psi}(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}) = \psi(\mathbf{v})(\mathbf{w})$$

Given $i\in \mathcal{I}$, for every $\mathbf{v}\in V_i$ and $\mathbf{w}\in W$, we have $$ \begin{alignat*}{2} \left(\overline{\psi}\circ \mathbf{j}_i\right)(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}) & = \overline{\psi}(\mathbf{i}_i(\mathbf{v})\otimes \mathbf{w}) \\ & = \psi(\mathbf{i}_i(\mathbf{v}))(\mathbf{w}) \\ & = (\psi \circ \mathbf{i}_i)(\mathbf{v})(\mathbf{w}) \\ & = \overline{\varphi}_i(\mathbf{v})(\mathbf{w}) \\ & = \varphi_i(\mathbf{v}\otimes \mathbf{w}) \end{alignat*} $$ so $\psi\circ \mathbf{j}_i = \varphi_i$. Since $i$ is arbitrarily choosen, $\left(V\otimes W, \{\mathbf{j}_i : V_i\otimes W\to V\otimes W\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}\right)$ is a direct sum of $\{V_i\otimes W\}_{i\in \mathcal{I}}$. Therefore $$\left(\bigoplus\limits_{i\in \mathcal{I}} V_i\right)\otimes W \cong V\otimes W \cong \bigoplus\limits_{i\in \mathcal{I}} \left(V_i\otimes W\right)$$
